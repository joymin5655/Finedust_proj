AirLens: Global Air Quality Intelligence

Democratizing access to air quality information through innovative machine learning and verified global data fusion.

Project Vision

AirLens was created to address a critical gap in global air quality monitoring. Existing systems often suffer from sparse data coverage, delayed public health warnings, and lack of real-time predictive capabilities. We believe everyone deserves to know the air they breathe, regardless of their location or technical expertise.

Our approach combines satellite imagery, ground-level sensor data, and historical policy analysis to provide accurate, hyper-local PM2.5 predictions directly in your browser, empowering individuals and researchers with actionable insights.

Research & Methodology

CNN-LSTM Architecture

Our hybrid Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) architecture extracts spatial features from satellite imagery, identifying patterns like cloud cover and aerosol density. LSTM layers process spatial data alongside time-series information from ground sensors and policy data, enabling the model to learn temporal dependencies and make accurate future predictions.

Multimodal Data Fusion

We implemented a Late Fusion architecture combining three data modalities: image features via CNN (40% weight), satellite remote sensing data (35% weight), and ground station measurements (25% weight). This leverages NASA MODIS for Aerosol Optical Depth (AOD), ESA Sentinel-5P for atmospheric composition (NO‚ÇÇ, CO, aerosol index), and OpenAQ for real-time ground truth within a 25km radius.

Key Features:
  • Multi-source data integration for enhanced accuracy
  • Weighted fusion based on data reliability
  • Cross-validation with ground stations when available
  • Global coverage via satellite data APIs

Triple Verification Pipeline

To ensure reliability, we developed a triple verification pipeline. Data is cross-validated against ground-truth sensor readings, compared with outputs from established atmospheric models like CAMS, and checked against satellite-derived aerosol optical depth (AOD) measurements. This multi-faceted validation process builds confidence in the model's output.
